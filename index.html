Anaplastic lymphoma kinase (ALK)

Fig 1. Known Ras signalling pathways1


Anaplastic lymphoma kinase (ALK) is a receptor tyrosine kinase that belongs to the insulin superfamily. ALK plays a role in the functioning of the nervous system as well as its development. Phosphorylated ALK activates several signalling pathways such as MAPK-ERK, PLC and PI3K-AKT. Abnormalities in the ALK pathway result in cancer development, such as anaplastic large cell lymphoma, neuroblastoma, or non-small lung cancer. Carcinogenesis results from ALK gene fusion with oncogenes, gene mutation, amplification, and an increase in protein expression. Usually, these genetic changes lead to constitutively active ALK receptor tyrosine kinase, consequently activating downstream signaling pathways and leading to uncontrolled cell growth, anti-apoptotic signaling, and cellular transformation. ALK can form a fusion protein with nucelophosmin (NPM) on the amino-terminal as a result of chromosomal translocation. Approximately 70% to 80% of anaplastic large cell lymphomas (ALCL) are associated with NPM-ALK. NPM-ALK activates the Ras-ERK signalling pathway which feeds into cell proliferation 2,3.
