Summary

From the analysis of the common structures of the Ras proteins, we have learned mutations at G12 and G13 of the P-loop make Ras oncogenic because they (the mutants) prevent the hydrolysis reaction. K16 makes H-bonds with the phosphates of the bound nucleotide to stabilize it during hydrolysis. S17 coordinates the Mg2+ ion, which is involved in the GTPase reaction. Therefore, the P-loop is fundamental for the correct binding of the nucleotide, which is necessary for GTP hydrolysis. Further, T35 is conserved in all GTP-binding proteins because it interacts, through its main chain –NH with the ϒ-phosphate of GTP, and through its side chain -OH group with the Mg2+ ion. Y32 interacts with the ϒ-phosphate through a bridging water molecule, so it is involved in stabilising switch I. F28 helps stabilise nucleotide binding through its side chain. Finally, G60 is conserved in all GTP binding proteins, because it interacts (along with T35 from switch I) with the ϒ-phosphate of GTP to create a “loaded spring mechanism”. Q61 is a highly conserved residue in the whole RAS superfamily because it is involved in the hydrolysis of the nucleotide triphosphate (it is also present in ATP binding proteins). Mutations at Q61 prevent the hydrolysis reaction and make Ras oncogenic.

BLAST searches in Homo sapiens, Mus musculus, Caenorhabditis elegans and Drosophila melanogaster identified a conserved Ras domain spanning the majority of H-Ras. This domain is responsible for hydrolysis of GTP to GDP.

The role of the Ras family proteins in cancer is an object of clinical intrest. The ultimate signalling result of the Ras family is cell growth and divsion, making its overactivity a subject of intrest in cancer research. The 3 Ras genes in humans are the most common oncogenes in cancer1. Mutations lead to activation Ras are found in 20% to 25% of all human tumors and up to 90% in certain types of cancer.
